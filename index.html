Module 01 · taught 08:45 — 09:30

Prompt Engineering essentials

~200 words is the sweet spot
Persona · Context · Task · Format
The foundation everything else stands on

Garbage in = Garbage out. The 4-part structure is your scaffolding. We start the repository here — and not with the tool tour — because everything else in this workshop assumes you can write a prompt. This module is the muscle-memory drill: we take a bad prompt, level it up to good, then to gold, and you'll feel the difference.

USE CASE 1.1
The "Bad → Good → Gold" rewrite drill
Any tool
Scenario

You need to write a job description for a Data Analyst role on the HR analytics team.

Level 1 · Bad
Write me a job description for a data analyst.
Level 2 · Good
Write a job description for a Data Analyst on the HR analytics team at an oil and gas company in Oman. Should include responsibilities, required skills, and qualifications.
Level 3 · Gold
See full prompt below ↓
Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste · the gold version 
Persona: Act as a senior HR talent acquisition specialist who has hired
data and analytics roles into corporate functions of large energy
companies in the GCC.

Context: We are hiring a Data Analyst (mid-level, ~5 years experience)
into the People Analytics team at an Omani oil and gas company. The role
sits inside HR, reports to the Head of People Analytics, and partners
with HR business partners and reward teams. Key responsibilities will
involve workforce planning models, attrition analysis, and dashboards
for senior leaders. The candidate pool we want to attract is Omani
nationals first, with openness to expats for niche skills. Strong
preference for candidates who already work in HR analytics, but we'll
consider strong analysts from finance or operations who want to move
into people data.

Task:
1. Write the job description with these sections: role summary, key
   responsibilities (6–8 bullets), required qualifications, preferred
   qualifications, what success looks like in the first 12 months.
2. After the JD, give me three suggested interview questions that would
   filter for the most important skill in this role.

Format: Professional, clear, GCC-appropriate language. Avoid Silicon
Valley clichés ("rockstar", "ninja", "10x"). Total length under 600
words. Output as plain text I can paste into our applicant tracking
system. No emojis.
The drill: Run all three levels in the same tool, back to back. The contrast is the lesson. Repeat this drill on three more tasks this week and the structure becomes automatic.
USE CASE 1.2
Negative constraints — telling AI what NOT to do
Claude
Scenario

You need to write a rejection email to internal candidates who didn't get a promotion. The default AI tone will be saccharine and full of "unfortunately." You want it warm but real.

⚠ Before you paste: Don't include candidate names or identifying details in a public AI tool. Write the template, then customize names yourself in your email client.
Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste 
Persona: Act as an HR business partner who is known internally for
delivering hard news with directness and respect.

Context: Three internal candidates applied for a promotion to a Senior
HR Specialist role. One was selected. I need to send the same core
rejection email to the two unsuccessful candidates. They are valued
employees we want to keep. They put effort into their applications.
They will be disappointed.

Task: Draft the rejection email.

Format and constraints:
- Maximum 180 words.
- Warm but direct. No sugar-coating.
- Do NOT use the word "unfortunately."
- Do NOT use phrases like "we had many strong candidates" or "this was
  a difficult decision."
- Do NOT promise specific future opportunities.
- DO acknowledge the effort they put in.
- DO offer one concrete next step (a 30-minute development conversation
  with their line manager).
- Sign-off: "Warm regards" — not "Best".
Why this works: Negative constraints are the most underused prompt technique. Telling the model what to avoid is often more effective than telling it what to do — it eliminates the average-case AI behavior you don't want.
USE CASE 1.3
Meta-prompting — AI writes its own prompt
ClaudeChatGPT
Scenario

You have a complex recurring task — a monthly board HR report — and you want a reusable, high-quality prompt template instead of starting from scratch each time.

Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste 
Persona: Act as a prompt engineering expert who specializes in designing
reusable prompt templates for corporate professionals.

Context: I produce a monthly HR board report. It always covers the same
sections: headcount movement, attrition rate, recruitment pipeline,
employee engagement signals, and one "spotlight" topic that changes each
month. My audience is the executive committee — five people with no
patience for fluff. Currently I draft this from scratch each month and
it takes me half a day.

Task: Design a reusable, parameterized prompt I can use every month. The
prompt should:
1. Accept the month's raw data as inputs (I'll paste in numbers and
   notes).
2. Produce the report in a consistent format that the exec committee
   can read in 5 minutes.
3. Include placeholders I fill in monthly, marked clearly with
   {{double_braces}}.
4. Include the persona, context, task, and format sections of a
   well-structured prompt.

Format: Output the reusable prompt in a code block I can copy. After the
prompt, give me a one-paragraph "how to use" guide.
Top-1% move: You're using AI to architect your own AI workflow. The output is an asset you'll reuse 12 times a year. Hours saved compound.

USE CASE 2.1
Same prompt, four tools, compare the output
Run in all four
Scenario

You've been asked to draft a one-page internal briefing on AI adoption risks for HR. You want to see how each tool handles the same request — so you know which one to default to next time.

⚠ Before you paste: Generic, public-information prompt — safe to run in any tool. We'll handle private data later.
Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste · 4-part structure 
Persona: Act as an experienced HR strategy consultant who has helped large
energy-sector organizations roll out AI tools to non-technical staff.

Context: I work in HR at a national energy company in Oman. Our workforce
is approximately 8,000 employees, predominantly Omani nationals, with a
mix of technical and corporate functions. Leadership is curious about AI
but cautious. We need a one-page internal briefing for the HR leadership
team that explains the top three adoption risks and how to mitigate each.
Assume readers are senior HR managers with no technical AI background.

Task:
1. Identify the three highest-priority adoption risks for an HR function
   introducing generative AI tools to non-technical staff.
2. For each risk, describe (a) what it looks like in practice, (b) why it
   matters, and (c) one concrete mitigation the HR team can own.
3. End with a 3-bullet "first 30 days" action list for the HR leadership
   team.

Format: One page maximum. Use a clear heading, three risk sections with
sub-bullets, and a final action list. Tone: pragmatic, plain English, no
hype, no jargon. No emojis.
Try this variation: After running it in all four tools, paste the four outputs back into Claude and ask: "Compare these four drafts. Score each 1–10 on clarity, specificity, and tone." You've just used AI to evaluate AI. This is a top-1% move.
USE CASE 2.2
Tool-selection self-test
ChatGPTClaude
Scenario

Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste 
Persona: Act as an AI-tools coach who knows the strengths and weaknesses
of ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and NotebookLM in detail as of 2026.

Context: I'm an HR professional with five tasks this week. For each task,
recommend which of the four tools I should use first, and explain in one

   line managers about it.
2. Draft a sensitive performance-improvement email to a senior employee.
3. Create a polished slide visual for a town hall on workforce planning.
4. Pull the latest published statistics on AI adoption in GCC HR

5. Build a quick interactive web form so managers can submit headcount

Try this variation: Reverse it — "For each tool, give me one HR task it would be the WRONG choice for." Knowing what each tool is bad at is more valuable than knowing what they're good at.

USE CASE 3.1
Inbox triage — 30 unread emails into a 5-minute morning brief
ClaudeGemini
Scenario

Monday morning. You open your inbox to 30+ unread emails. Some need action today, most don't. You want a triaged briefing in five minutes, not five hours.

⚠ Before you paste: Strip out salary figures, employee ID numbers, or named performance issues before pasting into a public tool. Replace names with [Manager A], [Employee B] if needed.
Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste 
Persona: Act as a sharp executive assistant who has triaged inboxes for
senior corporate leaders for years. You have a strong sense of what
actually matters versus what looks urgent.

Context: I'm pasting in the subject lines and first 2-3 lines of 30
emails from my inbox this morning. I have 90 minutes of focused work
time today before back-to-back meetings start. I need to know what
genuinely needs me today, what can wait, and what I can ignore or
delegate.

[PASTE EMAIL SUBJECTS AND PREVIEWS HERE]

Task:
1. Sort the emails into four buckets: ACT TODAY, RESPOND THIS WEEK,
   FYI ONLY, IGNORE / DELEGATE.
2. For each email in ACT TODAY, give me a one-line summary of what
   action is needed and an estimate of how long it will take.
3. Flag anything that looks like it's hiding something important behind
   a vague subject line.

Format: Four labeled sections with email subjects as bullets under each.
Action items as one-liners. No preamble. End with a single suggested
order in which to tackle the ACT TODAY items.

USE CASE 3.2
Meeting notes → action items in under 60 seconds
Claude
Scenario

You ran an HR planning meeting. You have rough notes or a Teams transcript. You need clear minutes and an action-item table you can send within an hour of the meeting ending.

Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste 
Persona: Act as a corporate chief of staff who is known for producing
minutes that are so clear and actionable that decisions never get
re-litigated in the next meeting.

Context: I'm pasting in the raw notes from a 60-minute HR planning
meeting. Attendees were the Head of HR, three HR business partners,
and a representative from Finance. The meeting covered next year's
hiring plan, succession planning for three senior roles, and a review
of the current performance management cycle. Notes are messy —
fragments, half-sentences, names without context.

[PASTE RAW NOTES OR TRANSCRIPT HERE]

Task:
1. Produce a clean meeting summary in three sections: Decisions made,
   Open questions parked for next meeting, Action items.
2. For action items, build a table with columns: Action | Owner | Due
   date | Notes.
3. If an action has no clear owner or due date in the notes, flag it
   with [OWNER UNCLEAR] or [DATE UNCLEAR] so I can chase it up.
4. At the end, list any contradictions or unresolved disagreements you
   noticed in the notes — even if I didn't flag them as open questions.

Format: Markdown. Clear heading hierarchy. Action item table must be a
real table, not bullet points. Total length under 400 words excluding
the table.
Replace [PASTE RAW NOTES OR TRANSCRIPT HERE] with this 
HR Planning meeting - Tuesday morning - rough notes
Attendees: Fatma (Head of HR), Khalid + Layla + Ibrahim (HRBPs), Saif (Finance)

  freeze and probably not even notice
- Ibrahim: pushback — Corp Services has 4 critical roles open, freeze
  hurts us more in 12 months than it saves
- discussion went in circles for 20 mins...
- Fatma: ok let's not decide today, need a priority ranking paper
- ACTION: Khalid to draft FY27 hiring priority paper, ranked by criticality
  not headcount. Due... she said next Wednesday I think? before exco
- Saif: also wants to see attrition assumption explicitly stated. fair point
- nobody disagreed but also nobody owned that piece

Succession - 3 senior roles

- GM Ops North - clear successor identified, Hassan, ready in 6 months
- Head of Reward - this is messy. Two candidates internal but neither ready,
  both think they are. Layla flagged retention risk if we go external
- Chief Engineer Subsea - no internal pipeline at all. external search needed
- Fatma: prefers internal where possible but won't compromise on quality
- Saif raised cost of external search ~ OMR 80K for the subsea role
- ACTION: Layla to have honest conversations with both Reward candidates
  about gaps. By end of next month
- ACTION: Ibrahim to scope external search for Subsea role
- timeline for Reward decision unclear — Fatma said "soon" but nothing firm

Performance management review

- Calibration sessions ran late in 4 of 8 clusters
- Manager comments quality - mixed. Some clusters excellent, others copy-paste

USE CASE 3.4
Quick analysis of an HR dataset
ChatGPTClaude
Scenario

You have last quarter's headcount and attrition data in a spreadsheet. Leadership wants a quick read on what's happening — not a report, just sharp observations.

⚠ Before you paste: Anonymize. Remove names, employee IDs, and salaries. Replace department names if they reveal individuals. If you have an enterprise/private AI tool, use that — it's the right tool for any data with PII.
Gold-standard prompt
Copy & paste · upload spreadsheet first 
Persona: Act as a senior people analytics consultant who has briefed
boards on workforce data for a decade. You know the difference between
a data point that's interesting and one that's actually decision-grade.

Context: I'm uploading a spreadsheet with quarterly headcount and
attrition data. Columns include: department, grade, quarter, headcount
start, joiners, leavers, voluntary vs involuntary, gender split,
nationality split (Omani vs expat). The HR leadership team will see
this on Monday. They have 10 minutes for it.

Task:
1. Identify the three most decision-grade observations in this data.
   "Decision-grade" means: someone in the room should do something
   different on Monday because of what we found.
2. For each observation, state (a) what the data shows, (b) why it
   matters, (c) one specific question the HR leadership team should
   discuss.
3. Flag any data quality issues you spotted — missing values,
   inconsistent categories, suspicious outliers.

Format: A short executive memo, max 350 words. Three observations as
numbered sections. Data quality issues at the end as a short bulleted
list. No charts in this output — just text and one summary table if
helpful.
Try this variation: After the response, ask: "Now generate the chart that best supports observation #1, and tell me what title to put on it." Raw data → insight → leadership-ready visual in three prompts.
Module 04 · 10:15 — 10:45

NotebookLM for document research

Grounded in your sources
Citations to specific pages
Will not hallucinate

NotebookLM is different from the other three tools in one critical way: it only knows what you upload. Every answer is footnoted to specific pages in your sources. This makes it the right tool — and often the only responsible tool — for any task involving company policies, contracts, manuals, or any documents where being wrong has consequences.

USE CASE 4.1
Build a "Policy Q&A bot" for line managers
NotebookLM
Scenario

Line managers constantly ping HR with questions like "How many days of bereavement leave are we entitled to?" or "What's the policy on remote work for new joiners?" You want them to self-serve.

Workflow
  1. Open NotebookLM (notebooklm.google.com)
  2. Create a new notebook called "HR Policy Reference"
  3. Upload: Employee Handbook, Leave Policy, Remote Work Policy, Code of Conduct, Performance Management Policy
  4. Use the prompt below to stress-test before sharing with managers
Gold-standard prompt
Paste into NotebookLM chat 
Persona: Act as an HR policy specialist who has worked with this
company's policies for years and answers line managers' questions
clearly and conservatively.

Context: I'm testing whether this notebook is reliable enough to share
with line managers as a self-service Q&A reference. I'm going to ask
five common manager questions. For each one, I need you to:

1. Answer based ONLY on the uploaded policy documents.
2. Cite the specific source and page number.
3. If the answer is unclear, partial, or contradicted across documents,
   say so explicitly — do not paper over it.
4. If a question genuinely cannot be answered from the documents, say
   "Not covered in the uploaded policies — escalate to the HR team."

The five test questions:
1. How many days of bereavement leave are employees entitled to?
2. Can a new joiner work remotely in their first three months?
3. What is the notice period for an employee resigning during probation?
4. Are line managers allowed to approve their own team's overtime?
5. What is the process for raising a grievance against a peer?

Format: For each question, show: Question / Answer / Source citation /
Confidence level (High, Medium, Low / Not covered).
Try this variation: Click NotebookLM's "Audio Overview" feature. It generates a 10-minute podcast-style conversation walking through your sources. Send it to new joiners during onboarding — the most painless way anyone has ever absorbed an employee handbook.
USE CASE 4.2

Part A · The Responsible AI checklist

Six things to check before you paste anything into a public AI tool:

  1. Is there PII? Names, employee IDs, salaries, performance ratings, medical info, contact details. If yes → strip, anonymize, or use a private/enterprise tool only.
  2. Is it confidential or commercially sensitive? Strategy decks, M&A discussions, contract terms, undisclosed financials. Public AI tools off-limits. Use sanctioned tools only.
  3. Could the output be wrong in a way that hurts someone? Policy answers, legal interpretations, medical advice, financial guidance. AI assists; a human decides.
  4. Am I about to act on output without verification? AI hallucinates with full confidence. Verify anything that goes in front of leadership, customers, or regulators.
  5. Did I label AI-generated content where it matters? For employee comms, training material, public-facing content — disclose where appropriate.
  6. Would I be comfortable if this prompt + output appeared on the internal news bulletin? If no, don't paste it.
